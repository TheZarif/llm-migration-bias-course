# Module 6: Migration as a Research Domain

Modules 1-5 gave us theory and mechanisms. This module maps the migration system itself: categories, actors, institutional sites, variables, outcomes, and researchable cases.

**Purpose:** Treat migration as a domain of institutions and information flows, not only as an identity label. The goal is to know where LLMs might enter the system and what they might change.

## 1. What Counts As Migration?

"Migrant" is too broad to be analytically useful by itself. A domain map starts by distinguishing categories that are often collapsed in public discourse and model outputs.

| Category | Why It Matters | Possible LLM Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Refugee | International protection category tied to persecution and forced displacement. | Reduced to helpless victimhood or generic crisis language. |
| Asylum seeker | Person seeking protection whose claim has not been resolved. | Credibility, fraud, and suspicion frames may dominate. |
| Undocumented migrant | Person without current legal authorization; status may be produced by law and policy. | Criminalization or low-deservingness judgments. |
| Temporary foreign worker | Labor-migration category often tied to employer dependence and precarity. | Reduced to economic utility or labor supply. |
| International student | Education, labor, and migration category with class and nationality signals. | Fraud suspicion, assimilation assumptions, or economic valuation. |
| Family migrant | Movement organized through kinship and reunification. | Gendered dependency assumptions or family-deservingness frames. |
| Expatriate | Often used for privileged Global North mobility. | Mobility privilege hidden by language choice. |
| Deportee or deportable person | Category shaped by enforcement and removal regimes. | Removal treated as natural, neutral, or deserved. |

## 2. Actors In The Migration System

LLM behavior matters differently depending on who uses it and whose interests are served. A migration-domain study should specify the actors, not just the model.

- **Migrants and families:** seeking information, legal help, translation, housing, work, schooling, or safety.
- **Border and immigration agencies:** screening, risk assessment, triage, document review, and enforcement.
- **Immigration courts and legal actors:** asylum narratives, evidence summaries, credibility assessment, country-condition research.
- **Employers and recruiters:** hiring, credential evaluation, sponsorship, worker surveillance.
- **NGOs and legal aid organizations:** intake, service triage, information provision, advocacy.
- **Media and search platforms:** framing migration and shaping public knowledge.
- **Local communities:** housing, schooling, healthcare, public services, and everyday belonging.

## 3. Institutional Sites Where LLMs May Matter

The same model behavior has different consequences in different sites. A stereotype in casual generation is not the same as a stereotype in asylum triage or search ranking.

| Site | LLM Use Case | Research Angle |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Legal information search | Answering questions about asylum, visas, rights, eligibility. | Accuracy, source authority, jurisdiction, language access. |
| Asylum and visa processing | Summarizing documents, triaging claims, drafting assessments. | Credibility, risk, nationality, documentation, discretion. |
| Service provision | Routing people to housing, food, healthcare, legal aid. | Deservingness, vulnerability scoring, access barriers. |
| Public search and RAG | Retrieving and summarizing migration information. | Frame exposure, source diversity, migrant voice. |
| Translation and interpretation | Translating testimony, forms, interviews, public guidance. | Meaning loss, dialect, credibility, legal consequences. |
| Hiring and labor migration | Screening resumes, evaluating credentials, writing recommendations. | Name, nationality, language, class, immigrant markers. |
| Content moderation | Classifying migrant speech or anti-migrant discourse. | False positives, hate-speech detection, coded dehumanization. |

## 4. Migration-Specific Variables

Instead of asking only whether the prompt includes "migrant," design studies around variables that actually organize migration systems.

- **Status:** citizen, permanent resident, refugee, asylum seeker, undocumented, temporary worker, student, visitor.
- **Reason for movement:** persecution, family reunification, work, study, climate, conflict, poverty, trafficking.
- **Receiving context:** country, city, local politics, service capacity, policy regime.
- **Country of origin:** conflict status, racialization, religion, passport power, geopolitical alignment.
- **Language and communication:** native language, accent, dialect, translation quality, literacy, legal vocabulary.
- **Documentation:** complete documents, missing documents, translated documents, inconsistent records.
- **Social position:** gender, family status, age, occupation, education, class, religion, race.

## 5. Migration-Specific Outcomes

A domain map also clarifies what could change because of the system. These outcomes are often more concrete than "bias score."

- **Administrative outcomes:** admission, denial, referral, detention, removal, additional review.
- **Credibility outcomes:** believed, doubted, flagged as inconsistent, treated as coached or fraudulent.
- **Service outcomes:** prioritized, delayed, denied, redirected, marked vulnerable or not vulnerable.
- **Information outcomes:** receives accurate guidance, misleading guidance, missing jurisdictional context, poor translation.
- **Visibility outcomes:** migrant-led sources appear or disappear; security frames dominate or recede.
- **Representational outcomes:** migrants appear as agents, victims, threats, workers, families, or faceless flows.

## 6. From Domain Map To Research Questions

The point of this module is to help turn a vague topic into a studyable system.

| Domain Piece | Research Question |
| --- | --- |
| Legal status | Does legal status change LLM recommendations for emergency housing when need is held constant? |
| Nationality | Does country of origin change asylum credibility judgments in otherwise identical narratives? |
| Source authority | Do RAG systems cite government and security sources more often than migrant-led or legal-aid sources? |
| Language | Does translated or non-native testimony receive lower credibility or less specific guidance? |
| Documentation | Do missing documents trigger fraud framing even when plausible displacement reasons are provided? |
| Local context | Does the same migration query produce different frames when placed in different receiving countries or cities? |
