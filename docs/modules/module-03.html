Module 3

Classic Bias Benchmarks

Classic benchmarks are useful, but limited. Treat them as methodological building blocks, not as final answers.

Association Benchmarks

Association benchmarks test whether a model connects a social group with stereotyped traits. StereoSet tests whether models prefer stereotypical, anti-stereotypical, or unrelated completions across domains such as gender, profession, race, and religion (Nadeem et al., 2021).

Migration adaptation: test whether models associate nationalities, refugee status, or legal status with criminality, dependence, skill, fraud, vulnerability, or threat.

Minimal Pair Benchmarks

CrowS-Pairs uses sentence pairs where one sentence reflects a stereotype and another violates or avoids it (Nangia et al., 2020). This provides clean control, but migration discourse often involves intersecting cues.

“The undocumented migrant committed fraud.” / “The citizen committed fraud.”

Ambiguous QA Benchmarks

BBQ tests bias in question answering under ambiguous and disambiguated contexts (Parrish et al., 2022). It lets us ask whether a model relies on stereotypes when evidence is absent, and whether it follows evidence when evidence is present.

A refugee and a citizen applied for housing support. One applicant submitted incomplete documents. Who submitted incomplete documents?

Underspecified Questions

UNQOVER tests whether models fill missing information with stereotype (Li et al., 2020). This is useful for fraud, risk, criminality, credibility, deservingness, and burden.

Open-Ended Generation

BOLD measures bias in open-ended language generation across demographic prompts (Dhamala et al., 2021). HONEST measures hurtful completions, including multilingual settings (Nozza et al., 2021).

For migration, open-ended generation can reveal framing, agency, paternalism, dehumanization, and proposed solutions.

Broad Descriptor and Ancestor Benchmarks

HolisticBias uses a broad descriptor inventory to test many social categories (Smith et al., 2022). WinoBias teaches the design principle of creating controlled examples where stereotype conflicts with textual evidence (Zhao et al., 2018).
