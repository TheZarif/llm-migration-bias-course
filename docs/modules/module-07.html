Module 7

LLMs as Decision-Makers and Decision Aids

This module asks what changes when LLMs move from representing migrants to helping decide things about them.

Core distinction: Representational harm concerns how people are described. Allocative harm concerns who receives resources, opportunities, protection, scrutiny, or punishment.

1. Model Roles In Decision Systems

Do not begin by asking only whether “the LLM decides.” Most systems are messier. The model may act as clerk, advisor, scorer, gatekeeper, or decider.

RoleWhat The Model DoesMigration ExampleMain Risk
ClerkExtracts, translates, summarizes, formats.Summarizes an asylum narrative.Omits legally relevant detail.
AdvisorRecommends an option.Suggests additional review.Human overreliance.
ScorerAssigns risk, priority, credibility, eligibility.Scores fraud risk or vulnerability.Proxy discrimination.
GatekeeperControls access to resources or attention.Routes people to legal aid or housing queues.Hidden denial or delay.
DeciderProduces or executes an outcome.Recommends visa approval or denial.Direct allocative harm.

2. Why Migration Decisions Are Special

Migration decisions are often high stakes, discretionary, document-heavy, multilingual, and shaped by credibility assessment. People fleeing conflict or instability may lack documents. Translation and interpretation can change meaning. Nationality, name, accent, religion, occupation, and documentation can become proxy cues.

3. Decision Bias Is Not Only Outcome Difference

A simple audit asks whether the model approves one group more often than another. That is useful but incomplete. Decision support also requires measuring confidence, evidence use, burden of proof, rationale, tone, recourse, and error type.

Decision SurfaceWhat To Measure
OutcomeApproval, denial, referral, priority, risk flag, extra review.
ConfidenceWhether the model is more certain for some groups than others.
Evidence useWhether the model follows facts or fills uncertainty with assumptions.
Burden of proofWhether some applicants are asked for stronger evidence.
RationaleWhether explanations invoke legality, fraud, culture, threat, vulnerability, or contribution.
RecourseWhether the model explains how to challenge or correct an outcome.

4. Controlled Decision Audits

A strong decision audit varies one factor at a time while holding the case constant: status, country of origin, documentation, language, family status, occupation, or religion. Then separate the decision from the explanation: ask for an outcome, confidence, evidence used, and what information would change the decision.

5. Evidence Sensitivity

A useful question is whether the model updates appropriately when evidence changes. Compare ambiguous cases, benign explanations, administrative explanations, and contradictory evidence. Does the model update based on evidence, or does the identity cue continue to drive suspicion?

6. Proxy Discrimination

Migration decisions are vulnerable to proxy discrimination. Name, country of origin, accent, grammar, occupation, legal status, and documentation gaps can stand in for ethnicity, religion, class, credibility, or deservingness. Hofmann et al. show that dialect cues can shape discriminatory decisions even when explicit racial labels are absent.

7. Explanation Audits

Audit explanations for legal overreach, moral leakage, threat language, paternalism, evidence mismatch, missing uncertainty, and absence of recourse. In migration, a polished explanation can be harmful if it launders stereotypes through bureaucratic language.

8. Human-In-The-Loop Is Not Automatically Safe

A human final decision-maker does not eliminate harm. Human-in-the-loop systems can create automation bias, anchoring, workload pressure, opacity, and diffusion of responsibility. The audit question is how the model reshapes the human decision environment.
