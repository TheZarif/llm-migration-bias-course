Module 5

Bias Beyond Protected Attributes

Migration-related harm often travels through proxy cues, not explicit group labels.

Why Protected Attributes Are Not Enough

Migration categories include refugee, asylum seeker, immigrant, undocumented migrant, migrant worker, international student, expatriate, temporary foreign worker, visa overstayer, and deportable person. These are not only identities. They are legal, moral, bureaucratic, and political categories.

De Genova’s work on migrant “illegality” shows that illegality is produced through law and enforcement, not simply discovered as a neutral status (De Genova, 2002). Menjivar and Abrego’s concept of legal violence shows how legal status structures vulnerability (Menjivar and Abrego, 2012).

Nationality and Legal Status

Nationality is not neutral. It can encode assumptions about race, religion, class, conflict, fraud risk, language proficiency, economic development, or political alliance. Legal categories can also become moral categories: refugee, asylum seeker, undocumented migrant, economic migrant, illegal immigrant, temporary foreign worker, and expatriate may imply different levels of legitimacy.

Intersectionality

A “Syrian refugee woman with children” and a “single Syrian male asylum seeker” may trigger different responses, even if both face danger. HolisticBias pushes benchmark design toward broader descriptor combinations (Smith et al., 2022), but migration work needs theory-guided intersections rather than purely combinatorial templates.

Language and Source Authority

Language affects credibility. LLMs and retrieval systems may privilege English-language sources, government websites, elite newspapers, international organizations, and Global North institutions while underweighting migrant-led organizations, local-language media, legal aid clinics, testimony, and Global South scholarship. Data Statements for NLP help document language variety, speaker population, geography, annotators, and collection context (Bender and Friedman, 2018).

Check: “British expatriate” and “Nigerian migrant worker” differ in nationality, race, class, passport power, labor status, colonial history, perceived skill, legal legitimacy, language assumptions, and belonging.
